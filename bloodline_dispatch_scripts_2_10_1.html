COLD OPEN — EXT. MORROW FARM, OZARK HIGHLANDS — PRE-DAWN
00:00
BLACK SCREEN. The sound of a horse breathing — slow, labored, wrong. Then silence.

FADE IN: A field at 4 AM. The Missouri sky is the color of a bruise. Fireflies have been replaced by something else — the soft blue glow of a phone flashlight cutting through knee-high grass.

We follow the light. Boots pressing into dew-soaked soil. Each step deliberate, searching.
AMBIENT: INSECT DRONE, LOW AND CONSTANT. SOMETHING IN THE GRASS.
The light finds it. A horse — 1,400 pounds of quarter horse — lying on its side. Not sleeping. The flank barely moving. Labored, shallow breaths.

A hand enters frame. Calloused. Steady. It touches the horse's jaw, gently, the way someone touches a thing they love and are afraid to lose.
CAL MORROW — V.O.
His name was Ranger. Fourteen years old. Champion cutting horse, two county fairs, never sick a day in his life. I'd delivered him myself — pulled him into the world on a January night just like this one.

I knew when I saw him that morning that I wasn't looking at colic. I wasn't looking at anything I'd treated before. I was looking at something new.
CUT TO: DR. CALHOUN "CAL" MORROW, 48. Face like a topographical map of everything he's been through. Weathered but not broken. He crouches beside Ranger, pulls a small headlamp from his jacket, begins examining the horse's neck, inner ears, legs.
CLOSE ON: Cal's gloved fingers parting the hair at Ranger's neck. He stops.
He leans closer. Then slower, he exhales through his nose. Not panic. Recognition. He's seen something he knows he shouldn't be seeing here.
EXTREME CLOSE UP: A cluster of ticks — dark, swollen, motionless. Dozens. Embedded in a ring pattern that is not random.
Do NOT explain the ring pattern. Let it sit. The audience will pause and rewatch this moment. Let the visual do the work.
