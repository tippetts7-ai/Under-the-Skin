THE CHANNEL
BLOODLINE DISPATCH
"BLOODLINE DISPATCH — Where the truth bites back."
Ten-word description: One vet. One billionaire. One tick. Zero coincidences.

Bloodline Dispatch sits at the intersection of investigative journalism and cinematic storytelling — a channel for audiences who distrust official narratives and hunger for deep-dive, character-driven exposés. The name carries a double meaning: the bloodline of corruption traced through powerful institutions, and the literal bloodsucking parasite at the center of the story. Every episode is shot and scored to feel like a prestige documentary, not a reaction video.

THE SILENCING

PROOF
Cal obtains internal documents proving the biotech program was expanded beyond cattle ticks — a second project, unauthorized, buried in a subsidiary's budget line. He goes public.

THE BITE
Six months later. Holt is photographed at his private estate — the same land where his childhood trauma began. He is diagnosed with alpha-gal syndrome. He can no longer eat meat. His doctors say it's permanent.
↳ HOOK: Poetic justice ending. Designed for viral clipping and comment explosion.

SCENE ONE
"THE DYING FIELD"
UNDER THE SKIN — EP. 01
Approx. Runtime ~8:00
Setting Ozark Highlands, MO
Tone Cinematic / Investigative
Cold open. No title card. No introduction. Throw the viewer into the scene mid-crisis. First 30 seconds must be visually arresting. Do not explain. Make them ask questions.
COLD OPEN — EXT. MORROW FARM, OZARK HIGHLANDS — PRE-DAWN00:00
BLACK SCREEN. The sound of a horse breathing — slow, labored, wrong. Then silence.

FADE IN: A field at 4 AM. The Missouri sky is the color of a bruise. Fireflies have been replaced by something else — the soft blue glow of a phone flashlight cutting through knee-high grass.

We follow the light. Boots pressing into dew-soaked soil. Each step deliberate, searching.
AMBIENT: INSECT DRONE, LOW AND CONSTANT. SOMETHING IN THE GRASS.
The light finds it. A horse — 1,400 pounds of quarter horse — lying on its side. Not sleeping. The flank barely moving. Labored, shallow breaths.

A hand enters frame. Calloused. Steady. It touches the horse's jaw, gently, the way someone touches a thing they love and are afraid to lose.
CAL MORROW — V.O.
His name was Ranger. Fourteen years old. Champion cutting horse, two county fairs, never sick a day in his life. I'd delivered him myself — pulled him into the world on a January night just like this one.

I knew when I saw him that morning that I wasn't looking at colic. I wasn't looking at anything I'd treated before. I was looking at something new.
CUT TO: DR. CALHOUN "CAL" MORROW, 48. Face like a topographical map of everything he's been through. Weathered but not broken. He crouches beside Ranger, pulls a small headlamp from his jacket, begins examining the horse's neck, inner ears, legs.
CLOSE ON: Cal's gloved fingers parting the hair at Ranger's neck. He stops.
He leans closer. Then slower, he exhales through his nose. Not panic. Recognition. He's seen something he knows he shouldn't be seeing here.
EXTREME CLOSE UP: A cluster of ticks — dark, swollen, motionless. Dozens. Embedded in a ring pattern that is not random.
Do NOT explain the ring pattern. Let it sit. The audience will pause and rewatch this moment. Let the visual do the work.

Episode One — "The Dying Field"
ACT ONE — EXT./INT. MORROW FARM — MORNING01:20
TIME CUT: Golden hour. The farm materializes in daylight. Rolling pasture, white-fenced paddocks, a modest clinic building attached to a converted barn. A hand-painted sign: MORROW LARGE ANIMAL VETERINARY — EST. 2011.

Ranger has been moved to the clinic. He's alive, barely. Cal moves with focused efficiency — IV line, blood draw, vitals log. A young vet tech, BRIANNA, 24, hovers nearby.
BRIANNA
(watching the blood draw)
You want me to call Dr. Hensley at the university extension?
CAL
(not looking up)
Not yet. I want my own numbers first before anyone else gets in this room.
BRIANNA
The Delacroix farm called. They've got two heifers showing similar symptoms. Fever, lethargy, won't eat.
Cal stops. Sets down the vial. Stares at the wall for exactly three seconds. This is a man who calculates before he reacts.
CAL
(quietly)
How far is Delacroix from here?
BRIANNA
Four miles. Maybe five.
Cal writes something on his notepad. His face betrays nothing. But the pen presses hard enough to tear the paper.
CAL MORROW — V.O.
Here's what they don't teach you in veterinary school: the difference between an outbreak and an epidemic is usually just the number of phone calls you get before noon. By 10 AM that day, I had four.
RAPID MONTAGE — MULTIPLE FARMS, OZARK REGION — DAY ONE02:45
VISUAL SEQUENCE — HAND-HELD, DOCUMENTARY STYLE. EACH SHOT: 3-4 SECONDS.
— A goat lying in a feed pen, glassy-eyed.
— An elderly farmer, MERLE, standing at a fence line, hat in hand, watching his cattle bunch at the far end of the pasture like they're afraid of something closer.
— Cal's truck, dust plume behind it, pushing down a gravel road at speed.
— A dead dog near a tree line. Owner crouched beside it, not understanding.
— Cal's hands, latex-gloved, parting animal fur. Finding what he already expects to find.
— A map on his dashboard, red dots appearing in marker. Four farms. Five miles radius. The dots form a shape. Cal stares at it. Doesn't like what he sees.
SCORE: LOW DRONE BUILDS. SINGLE SUSTAINED CELLO NOTE.
INT. MORROW FARM CLINIC — INTERVIEW SETUP — LATER THAT DAY04:00
DIRECT TO CAMERA. CAL SEATED. CLINICAL BACKGROUND SLIGHTLY SOFT. THIS IS THE "NOW" TIMELINE.
CAL MORROW
(deliberate, controlled)
Fourteen years I've worked this region. I've seen Lyme clusters. I've seen anaplasmosis roll through a herd. I've seen what happens when tick populations spike after a mild winter — and it looks nothing like what I saw that day.

What I saw looked systematic. The distribution pattern across those five farms didn't follow any natural gradient I'd ever studied. Ticks don't respect property lines, but they also don't show up in uniform density across multiple unconnected properties simultaneously. That's — that's not ecology. That's placement.
He lets that word sit. He's a scientist. He chose it carefully.
At 4:00 — drop a lower-third graphic: "Dr. Cal Morrow has reported his findings to three separate state agencies. None have responded." This creates institutional distrust and viewer outrage. Keep it on screen for 5 seconds.
INT. MORROW FARM CLINIC — LATE EVENING — DAY ONE05:10
Cal sits on an overturned bucket beside Ranger's stall. The horse's breathing has steadied but he's not out of danger. Cal holds the blood panel results in his hands. He's been reading them for a long time.

Brianna appears in the doorway.
BRIANNA
You should eat something.
CAL
(not looking up)
Babesiosis markers are all wrong. It reads like babesiosis but it's not presenting the way babesiosis presents. The pathogen response is —
(pauses)
It's like something that's been modified to be harder to identify.
BRIANNA
(a beat)
Modified how?
CAL
(finally looks up)
That's what I intend to find out.
SCORE: SINGLE PIANO NOTE. HELD.
EXT. MORROW FARM — NIGHT — END OF DAY ONE06:45
Cal stands on his porch. A glass of water in his hand, untouched. He's staring at the tree line — the dark edge of his property where his field meets the forest.

He pulls out his phone. Scrolls. Finds an old news article: a biotech grant announcement. A foundation name he recognizes. A familiar face in the photo.

He reads it once. Then again.
CAL MORROW — V.O.
I'd spent six years at Velacor Pharmaceuticals before I walked away. I know what institutional money looks like. I know what research looks like when it's meant to solve a problem — and I know what it looks like when the research IS the problem.

That night, standing on my porch in the dark, I found a thread. I didn't know yet how far it would unravel. I didn't know what it would cost me.

But I pulled it.
SLOW PUSH IN ON CAL'S FACE. THE TREE LINE BEHIND HIM, OUT OF FOCUS. SOMETHING IN THE GRASS CATCHES THE PORCH LIGHT — BRIEF, BARELY VISIBLE. A BOX. PARTIALLY HIDDEN BY BRUSH.
Cal doesn't see it yet.

But we do.
